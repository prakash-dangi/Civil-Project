Theory of Deformation and Young's Modulus

In material science and engineering, the deformation (\( \delta \)) in a simple bar under an applied force (\( F \)) can be calculated using Hooke's Law:

\[ \delta = \frac{F \cdot L_0}{A \cdot E} \]

\[ \mu = -\frac{\varepsilon_{\text{transverse}}}{\varepsilon_{\text{axial}}} \]

Where: - \( \delta \) is the deformation, - \( F \) is the applied force, - \( L_0 \) is the initial/original length, - \( A \) is the cross-sectional area, - \( E \) is Young's Modulus.

Hooke's Law states that the deformation of an elastic material is directly proportional to the applied force within the elastic limit. Young's Modulus (\( E \)) represents the material's stiffness or rigidity.

The formula can be rearranged to solve for Young's Modulus (\( E \)):

\[ E = \frac{F \cdot L_0}{A \cdot \delta} \]

This equation provides a crucial understanding of how materials respond to applied forces, enabling engineers to design structures and predict material behavior under various conditions.
